REAL SECTION

Bees play a fundamental role in global agriculture by pollinating more than 70% of the crops humans rely on for food, including fruits, vegetables, nuts, and seeds. Without bees, food production would drop significantly, threatening food security and biodiversity. Pollination also affects livestock, as many animal feed crops depend on bee activity. Protecting bee populations is critical for sustaining life on Earth.



Water covers approximately 71% of Earth's surface, with oceans accounting for about 97% of that water. Oceans regulate the climate, support marine biodiversity, and provide resources such as fish and salt. They also absorb carbon dioxide, mitigating some effects of climate change, and influence weather patterns around the world.



The Great Wall of China, stretching over 21,000 kilometers, was constructed over several dynasties to protect Chinese states from invasions. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most extensive architectural projects in human history, demonstrating ancient engineering skills and military strategy.



Mount Everest, located in the Himalayas, is the tallest mountain on Earth, with a peak at 8,848.86 meters above sea level. First summited in 1953 by Sir Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay, it attracts climbers from around the world. Everest's glaciers are vital sources of freshwater for millions of people in South Asia.



The human heart pumps around 5 liters of blood per minute while at rest, circulating blood through the body to supply oxygen and nutrients to tissues. A healthy heart beats roughly 60 to 100 times per minute, and maintaining cardiovascular health is crucial to preventing heart disease, the leading cause of death globally.



The Amazon Rainforest, spanning over 5.5 million square kilometers across South America, is home to approximately 10% of the world's known species. It plays a crucial role in regulating global climate, sequestering carbon dioxide, and maintaining the water cycle, making it essential for biodiversity and climate stability.



Lightning is a discharge of electricity that occurs between clouds or between clouds and the ground. It heats the air to temperatures around 30,000 Kelvin, producing thunder. Lightning is responsible for starting natural wildfires and is a critical component of Earth's atmospheric nitrogen cycle.



Sharks have existed for more than 400 million years, predating dinosaurs. They are apex predators in marine ecosystems, maintaining the balance of species below them in the food chain. Many shark species are now threatened due to overfishing and habitat loss.



The ozone layer in Earth's stratosphere absorbs most of the Sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation. Its depletion, caused primarily by chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), increases the risk of skin cancer and cataracts in humans and affects plant and marine ecosystems. International agreements like the Montreal Protocol have helped reduce ozone-depleting emissions.



The speed of light in a vacuum is approximately 299,792 kilometers per second. It is a fundamental constant in physics and is used to define the meter, the standard unit of length. The speed of light affects the propagation of electromagnetic waves, influencing communication technologies and astronomical observations.



The Pacific Ocean is the largest ocean on Earth, covering about 63 million square miles. It contains the Mariana Trench, the deepest known point in the world's oceans, reaching a depth of approximately 10,924 meters. The Pacific Ocean supports diverse marine life and is vital for global trade and climate regulation.



DNA, or deoxyribonucleic acid, contains the genetic instructions used in the development and functioning of all known living organisms. Discovered by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953, the double-helix structure of DNA is central to modern genetics, enabling advances in medicine, forensics, and biotechnology.



The Eiffel Tower in Paris, France, was completed in 1889 and stands at 324 meters tall. Initially criticized, it has become a global cultural icon and a major tourist attraction. The structure demonstrates advances in iron construction techniques and engineering of the late 19th century.



Honey never spoils. Archaeologists have found pots of honey in ancient Egyptian tombs over 3,000 years old that are still edible. Its long shelf life is due to low water content and high acidity, which inhibit the growth of bacteria and microorganisms.



Venus is the second planet from the Sun and has an average surface temperature of approximately 464°C, making it the hottest planet in the solar system. Its dense atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide creates a runaway greenhouse effect, leading to extreme surface pressure and temperatures.



The first programmable computer, called the Z3, was created by Konrad Zuse in 1941. It used binary floating-point arithmetic and could perform automatic calculations based on stored instructions. Zuse's invention laid the groundwork for modern computing technology.



Shakespeare's play "Romeo and Juliet," written in the late 16th century, explores themes of love, fate, and family conflict. It is one of the most performed plays worldwide and has influenced literature, theatre, and popular culture across centuries.



The human brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons, which communicate through synapses using chemical and electrical signals. Neural networks are responsible for memory, cognition, motor control, and emotions, making brain health crucial for overall human functioning.



The Statue of Liberty, gifted by France to the United States in 1886, stands on Liberty Island in New York Harbor. It symbolizes freedom and democracy. The statue is made of copper, and its green color is the result of natural patination over time.



Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water into glucose and oxygen. It sustains life on Earth by providing energy for plants and producing the oxygen necessary for animal respiration.



The Sahara Desert is the largest hot desert in the world, covering approximately 9 million square kilometers. Its arid climate and extreme temperatures shape unique ecosystems, including specialized plants and animals adapted to survive in harsh conditions.



Alexander Graham Bell was awarded the first US patent for the telephone in 1876. His invention revolutionized communication and laid the foundation for the modern telecommunications industry.



The human skeleton is composed of 206 bones in adulthood. Bones provide structural support, protect internal organs, store minerals, and house bone marrow, which produces blood cells essential for life.



The Richter scale, developed by Charles F. Richter in 1935, measures the magnitude of earthquakes. Each whole number increase represents a tenfold increase in measured amplitude and roughly 31.6 times more energy release.



Penguins are flightless birds primarily found in the Southern Hemisphere. Emperor penguins, the tallest and heaviest species, can dive over 500 meters to hunt for fish and krill, surviving in extremely cold Antarctic conditions.



Albert Einstein developed the theory of relativity, which transformed physics by describing the relationship between space, time, and gravity. Published in 1905, the special theory of relativity introduced concepts such as E=mc² and time dilation.



The human liver performs over 500 vital functions, including detoxification, protein synthesis, and regulation of blood sugar. It is the largest internal organ and plays a central role in maintaining homeostasis.



The Dead Sea, located between Jordan and Israel, is the lowest point on Earth's surface at approximately 430 meters below sea level. Its high salt concentration allows people to float effortlessly and creates a unique mineral-rich environment.



Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, with a diameter of about 139,820 kilometers. It has a strong magnetic field, dozens of moons, and a complex system of rings, influencing the orbital dynamics of other planets and asteroids.



The Panama Canal, completed in 1914, connects the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans, significantly reducing maritime travel time. It is a critical infrastructure for global trade, demonstrating remarkable engineering and logistical planning.
